Getting Into Development

How I became a developer in three years

Getting started as a developer is no easy task; it takes hard work, a lot of learning, persistence, some creativity, and sometimes a little luck.

I’ve spent most of my professional developer career as a COBOL developer, albeit only four years. Before this, I was a systems admin for eight years.

I first started COBOL in 2014. I was amazed at the lack of information online about how to do anything in this language. You have to read the documentation to figure it out.

Because my job was split between dev and admin, I didn’t have much of an opportunity to write code. I spent most of my evenings and weekends trying to learn how to write COBOL.

Around late 2014 I still hadn’t had much of an opportunity to learn COBOL, but I was getting interested in iOS development with Objective-C. This was very different from what I was used to but I liked it much better than COBOL for all of the built-in functionality provided by the standard library.

It was also around this time when work started picking up and they asked me to write an application for Windows.

I started my trek by purchasing a course to learn C#. I learned a lot on my first run through, but mostly about structuring code and performing small tasks.

I had my foundation of variables and constants, conditional statements, basic expressions, and an understanding of what the standard library contained and what could be expected in other languages.

On my second run through the course, I learned more about the various domain layers of applications and structuring applications for future modifications. By the end of it, I was able to write simple applications to get work done.

Before moving on to another job in 2017, I was able to write a gateway monitoring application that would switch the network over to a backup system as a failover.

In the meantime, I continued learning. In 2015 I started with Swift. I enjoyed the language but it was hard to learn. Not because of the syntax but because I had all of these preconceived ideas about how C# was doing work and I was essentially trying to write Swift as I would C#.

Once I recognized this, I started reading up on how to properly structure my code in Swift, how to leverage some of the ideas that were unique to Swift to improve my coding skills.

It wasn’t until I decided to try and write a full iOS application, complete with views, databases, and networking, that I started to understand the language.

Getting Into Design

Along the way, I ended up taking a crash course in design, not just user interfaces (UI) but also taking into consideration user experience (UX).

Apple asks its developers to meet a set of human interface guidelines. These require each developer to adhere to rules that ensure a similar UI/UX exists between all apps.

When getting into design, I started learning about colors, shades, harmony, the bezier pen tool, and various vector and photo design programs. It took about a year to start making designs that I didn’t think were trash; however, my architecture skills were still less than desired.

Back to the Foundations

At one point while learning Swift, I got stuck, a piece of code wasn’t working correctly, and I had no idea why.

I knew that most object-oriented languages today come from implementations done in C++. Since C++ is spun off of C, I decided to try and learn C instead. I figured, if I went back to the foundations of modern programming, I would have clarity on what I should be doing.

I audited the Harvard’s CS50X on edx.org to point me in the right direction. I didn’t feel that I needed a completion certificate as I already had “developer” on my resume.

The first few lessons just introduced me to the language on topics I already knew. However, when I came to the subject of pointers, my mind melted. I thought: “This is freaking hard,” and it was.

It took me about five weeks of research to finally understand what pointers were, why they were necessary, and how they were used. Once I realized this one concept, things in C# and Swift made sense. I continued the course until I got to the PHP section (now Python) and switched gears back to Swift.

Newfound Clarity

I attacked my application with newfound clarity. Looking over my code, I would say: “This is shit” and replace it with something more performant or something more explicit.

Understanding pointers made me understand more about classes, arrays, and dictionaries, even if they are value types in Swift. Again, going back to the roots of object-oriented programming (OOP) made me think of things in a brand new light.

It’s now late 2016, early 2017, and I’m looking at extending my application with things like JSON and XML serialization.

In my professional career, I was getting tired of being an admin on the phone all the time. My creative side was chomping at the bit to develop new things.

In mid-2017, I took a job at my current company, where I started as a COBOL developer. The difference now was that I had a better understanding of program structure, program flow, and both OOP and structured language programming.

I would consider this to be my first developer job as I spent more time as an admin in my previous position.

I followed the adage “fake it until you make it” and I made it. It took a lot of nights and weekends and many free courses. I only purchased one for $150 and found the material about the same as the free courses.

New Passion

With this job came an agreement that anything I worked out outside of the company became intellectual property (IP) of the company.

Because of this, I halted progress on my iOS application and focused more on work. One thing that didn’t change was learning something new every night. Whether in C, C#, Swift, or COBOL didn’t matter as long as I learned something.

I finally felt like I had a passion for what I did. I wasn’t distracted by phones anymore and I didn’t mind working over as a salaried employee because of the work environment. This was when I discovered that a company’s culture matters.

The typical culture I’ve experienced is everything short of forced labor. Managers would micromanage or be distrusting of employees, which created a hostility in the environment that was hard to pinpoint, but the feeling was palpable.

If you work somewhere with this environment and you don’t believe in the product or service; my suggestion is to find another job. You’ll be much happier.

Object-Oriented Programming

In mid-2018, I grew tired of structure programming, while I didn’t have a problem writing COBOL, I wanted to do OOP again.

I missed the pre-built functions and classes from the standard libraries. I didn’t want to leave the company, even with the rule about IP in place, so I looked in other departments that were not writing COBOL.

I found an opening for a Linux administrator. It was a little more than just an administrator; it was a position in DevOps, more focused on the developer side writing Python. Here was my chance to get back to OOP languages and learn new skills.

I got the job and was immediately placed on a large project written in Python.

I picked up Python relatively quickly, roughly two weekends, and I was proficient enough to write a broad application. I think what sped up my learning was how similar the standard library is to Swift.

While writing this application, I had a crash course in JavaScript.

Never having touched it before, this was difficult. I ended up leveraging jQuery and learned a bit about async requests to update the UI as various options were selected.

It was also when writing this application that I wrote a good architecture.

I learned to leverage frameworks to speed up development and make my life a little easier. The only downside is that every framework is different, so you have to learn how to use each one.

While in my new position, I’ve also picked up Markdown, YAML, Perl, and Go while improving my PowerShell, Bash, and batch scripting.

The most important thing you can do as a developer is to keep your tools sharp and relevant. The learning never ends.
